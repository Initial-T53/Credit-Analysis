# Credit-Analysis
Credit Analysis dashboard looking at applicant demographic data and their credit history.

## Project Overview:
A regional lending manager approached me with a recurring problem: his team was reviewing hundreds of credit applications each week, but they had no clear way to identify risk patterns, understand applicant behavior, or prioritize files that required deeper scrutiny. He asked me to build a data‑driven solution that could surface early warning signs, highlight high‑risk segments, and give his analysts a clearer picture of each applicant’s credit history.

I designed a multi‑page credit‑risk dashboard that consolidates demographic factors, payment behavior, credit utilization, and delinquency patterns into a single analytical workflow. The goal was to help the lending team move from reactive decision‑making to proactive risk assessment, reducing manual review time while improving consistency and defensibility in credit decisions.

## Final Conclusion
The applicant portfolio is large, diverse, and generally stable, with strong repayment behavior across most demographic groups. Despite a broad mix of income levels, education backgrounds, and employment histories, overall delinquency and default rates remain low. Risk is not evenly distributed, however. Middle-income applicants and individuals with lower education levels show consistently higher default rates, and certain housing situations (such as renting or non-owned living arrangements) correlate with elevated risk.
High-income applicants, while financially capable, also exhibit a surprisingly higher relative delinquency rate, suggesting that credit exposure—not income alone—may be driving repayment challenges. Meanwhile, applicants with longer employment histories and higher education levels demonstrate the strongest repayment performance.
Taken together, the dashboard highlights a portfolio that is fundamentally healthy but benefits from targeted underwriting attention. Strengthening review processes for middle-income, lower-education, and non-homeowner applicants—while maintaining current criteria for stable, long-tenured earners—can improve risk consistency without restricting overall credit growth.
